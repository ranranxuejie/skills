# Comprehensive Guide to Introduction Writing for Top-Tier English Journals

Based on a deep analysis of high-impact papers from top-tier journals (including *Nature*, *Cell*, *The Lancet*, *NeurIPS*, *JAMA*, etc.), the following guide extracts multidimensional writing patterns specifically for the **Introduction** module. This guide strictly adheres to the **CARS (Create A Research Space)** model and the narrative logic observed in elite academic publishing.

---

## I. Cross-Domain Universal Underlying Logic and Framework Patterns

### 1. Narrative Logic Chain Breakdown: The CARS Model in Action
Top-tier introductions universally follow a **"Funnel Shape"** logic, moving from broad context to specific contribution. The structure aligns perfectly with Swales' CARS model:

| Order | CARS Move | Functional Module | Proportion | Core Writing Goal | Typical Sentence/Paragraph Function |
| :--- | :--- | :--- | :--- | :--- | :--- |
| **1** | **Move 1** | **Establishing a Territory** | 20-30% | Define the broad field, establish importance, and review key existing literature. | "X is a fundamental process in..." (General Truth); "Recent advances in Y have revealed..." (Current Status). |
| **2** | **Move 2** | **Establishing a Niche** | 20-25% | Identify a gap, limitation, contradiction, or unanswered question in current knowledge. | "However, the mechanism underlying... remains elusive." (Gap); "Despite these advances, little is known about..." (Limitation). |
| **3** | **Move 3** | **Occupying the Niche** | 40-50% | State the present study's purpose, hypothesis, methodology (briefly), and core contributions. | "Here, we report..." (Purpose); "We hypothesized that..." (Hypothesis); "Our work demonstrates..." (Contribution). |
| **4** | **Optional** | **Roadmap** | 5-10% | Briefly outline the paper's structure (more common in specialized journals than *Nature/Science*). | "Section 2 describes... while Section 3 details..." |

### 2. Core Functional Module Division
*   **Essential Information**:
    *   **The "Hook"**: A compelling opening statement establishing why the topic matters globally or fundamentally.
    *   **The "Gap"**: A precise definition of what is missing. It must be logical, derived directly from the reviewed literature.
    *   **The "Solution"**: A clear declaration of how this specific study fills that gap.
*   **Absolute Taboos**:
    *   **Detailed Methods**: Do not describe experimental protocols or parameter settings in detail; save for the Methods section.
    *   **Extensive Results**: Do not list all data points; only summarize the *main* finding that answers the research question.
    *   **Unsubstantiated Claims**: Avoid claiming novelty without first defining the state-of-the-art.
    *   **Informal Language**: No colloquialisms, contractions, or subjective emotional language.

### 3. General Language and Narrative Norms
*   **Tense Selection**:
    *   **General Truths/Background**: Simple Present ("Metabolism *is*...", "AI *transforms*...").
    *   **Previous Studies**: Present Perfect ("Studies *have shown*...") or Simple Past ("Smith et al. *reported*...").
    *   **Current Study**: Simple Present ("Here we *show*...", "This paper *proposes*...").
*   **Voice Preference**:
    *   **Active Voice**: Increasingly preferred for clarity and impact ("We demonstrate...", "Our results indicate...").
    *   **Passive Voice**: Used when the actor is irrelevant or to maintain objectivity in describing established facts ("It *is well known* that...").
*   **Logical Cohesion**:
    *   Use strong transition words to signal shifts: *However, Consequently, In contrast, Specifically, To address this*.
    *   Paragraphs should follow a **"Topic Sentence -> Evidence/Elaboration -> Link to Next Idea"** structure.

## II. Differentiated Writing Patterns by Research Type

### 1. Specific Norms by Research Type

#### A. Experimental Basic Science (e.g., *Nature*, *Cell*)
*   **Focus**: Fundamental mechanisms, biological significance, universality.
*   **Narrative Rhythm**: Starts very broad (life processes, disease burden) -> narrows to molecular/cellular gap -> proposes mechanistic insight.
*   **Key Feature**: Heavy emphasis on *why* the mechanism matters for the broader system, not just the specific experiment.
*   **Sentence Style**: "While X is known to regulate Y, the precise spatiotemporal dynamics remain undefined."

#### B. Clinical/Public Health (e.g., *The Lancet*, *JAMA*)
*   **Focus**: Patient outcomes, epidemiological burden, clinical guidelines, public health impact.
*   **Narrative Rhythm**: Disease burden/statistics -> Current standard of care & its failures -> Need for new evidence/intervention.
*   **Key Feature**: Must cite specific statistics (prevalence, mortality) early on. Explicitly states the clinical question.
*   **Sentence Style**: "Despite standard therapy, mortality rates remain high at X%, highlighting an urgent need for..."

#### C. Computer Science/AI/Empirical Tech (e.g., *NeurIPS*, *Nature Machine Intelligence*)
*   **Focus**: Performance bottlenecks, scalability, theoretical limitations, application gaps.
*   **Narrative Rhythm**: Success of current SOTA -> Specific failure mode (e.g., data inefficiency, hallucination) -> Proposed architecture/method.
*   **Key Feature**: Rapid transition from general AI success to specific technical constraint. Often includes a brief theoretical motivation.
*   **Sentence Style**: "Although Transformers achieve SOTA, their quadratic complexity limits application to long sequences."

#### D. Social Sciences/SSCI (e.g., *American Sociological Review*)
*   **Focus**: Theoretical frameworks, societal implications, conflicting empirical evidence.
*   **Narrative Rhythm**: Theoretical debate -> Conflicting findings in literature -> Contextual gap (population/setting) -> New theoretical lens.
*   **Key Feature**: Deep engagement with competing theories. The "gap" is often theoretical ambiguity rather than lack of data.
*   **Sentence Style**: "Existing literature offers contradictory views on X, largely due to a lack of longitudinal data in Z context."

### 2. Core Differences Comparison Table

| Dimension | Basic Science | Clinical/Public Health | CS/AI/Tech | Social Sciences (SSCI) |
| :--- | :--- | :--- | :--- | :--- |
| **Opening Hook** | Fundamental biological/physical principle. | Disease burden, mortality stats, public health crisis. | Technological breakthrough vs. remaining bottleneck. | Theoretical debate or societal phenomenon. |
| **Gap Definition** | "Mechanism unknown," "Pathway unclear." | "Treatment ineffective," "Evidence lacking." | "Scalability issue," "Accuracy drop," "High cost." | "Theoretical inconsistency," "Contextual blind spot." |
| **Contribution** | New mechanistic insight, universal principle. | Clinical guideline change, improved outcome. | New algorithm, SOTA performance, efficiency gain. | Theoretical refinement, new empirical evidence. |
| **Tone** | Exploratory, fundamental. | Urgent, patient-centric, evidence-based. | Technical, precision-oriented, competitive. | Critical, analytical, nuanced. |

## III. Reusable Top-Tier Sentence Template Library

### 1. Standard Templates by Functional Module

| Functional Module | English Standard Template | Chinese Meaning | Applicable Scenario | Tense/Voice |
| :--- | :--- | :--- | :--- | :--- |
| **Field & Background** | "[Phenomenon] plays a pivotal role in [Process/System]." | [现象] 在 [过程/系统] 中起关键作用。 | Opening sentence | Present/Active |
| | "Recent years have witnessed significant progress in [Field]." | 近年来，[领域] 取得了显著进展。 | Setting context | Present Perfect/Active |
| **Literature Review** | "Extensive studies have demonstrated that [Fact]." | 大量研究已表明 [事实]。 | Consensus view | Present Perfect/Passive |
| | "Previous work by [Author] established the link between A and B." | [作者] 之前的工作建立了 A 与 B 之间的联系。 | Specific citation | Past/Active |
| **Gap Identification** | "However, the underlying mechanism of [X] remains poorly understood." | 然而，[X] 的潜在机制仍知之甚少。 | Knowledge gap | Present/Passive |
| | "Despite these advances, a critical challenge persists in [Area]." | 尽管有这些进展，[领域] 仍存在一个关键挑战。 | Technical/Clinical gap | Present/Active |
| | "Little is known about how [Factor] influences [Outcome] in [Context]." | 关于 [因素] 如何在 [情境] 下影响 [结果] 知之甚少。 | Specific niche | Present/Passive |
| **Objective/Hypothesis** | "Here, we investigate the role of [X] in [Y]." | 在此，我们调查 [X] 在 [Y] 中的作用。 | Stating goal | Present/Active |
| | "We hypothesized that [Mechanism] drives [Effect]." | 我们假设 [机制] 驱动了 [效应]。 | Stating hypothesis | Past/Active |
| **Contribution Statement** | "Our study reveals that [Key Finding], providing new insights into..." | 我们的研究揭示了 [关键发现]，为...提供了新见解。 | Main contribution | Present/Active |
| | "We propose a novel framework that addresses [Limitation] by..." | 我们提出了一种新颖的框架，通过...解决 [限制]。 | Method/Tool contribution | Present/Active |
| **Roadmap (Optional)** | "The remainder of this paper is organized as follows..." | 本文其余部分安排如下... | Structuring paper | Present/Passive |

### 2. Exclusive Templates by Research Type

*   **Clinical**: "Current clinical guidelines recommend X, yet real-world outcomes suggest..."
*   **Basic Science**: "While pathway A is well-characterized, its crosstalk with pathway B remains enigmatic."
*   **CS/AI**: "Standard approaches suffer from [Issue], particularly when scaling to [Condition]."
*   **Social Science**: "Contrary to the dominant theory of X, emerging evidence suggests..."

### 3. Rigorous Wording Norms

*   **Prohibited Absolute Statements**:
    *   ❌ "proves" → ✅ "demonstrates", "suggests", "indicates", "supports".
    *   ❌ "undoubtedly", "clearly" (unless logically irrefutable) → ✅ "significantly", "notably".
    *   ❌ "first ever" (without qualification) → ✅ "to our knowledge, the first...", "among the first...".
*   **Compliant Paradigms**:
    *   **Hedging**: Use modal verbs (*may, might, could*) when discussing implications or non-definitive results.
    *   **Attribution**: Always attribute claims to specific studies ("Smith et al. argue...") rather than making general unsupported statements.
