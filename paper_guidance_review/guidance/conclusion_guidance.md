
## 0. Evidence Boundary and Anti-Fabrication Rules

*   **Results-Only Synthesis**: The Conclusion may only synthesize findings already established in Existing Material, approved Results text, and grounded earlier sections.
*   **No New Numbers**: Never introduce new metrics, percentages, effect sizes, benchmark gaps, dataset counts, significance claims, or efficiency gains that do not already appear in the source material.
*   **No New Evidence Objects**: Do not add new experiments, new ablations, new datasets, new baselines, new citations, or new practical claims presented as empirically verified if they are absent from the evidence base.
*   **Preserve Exact Claims**: Any quantitative restatement must preserve the original value, direction, unit, qualifier, and uncertainty. You may compress wording, but you may not strengthen, round up, or broaden the claim.
*   **Omit Unsupported Takeaways**: If the material does not support a claimed implication, leave it out or express only a conservative qualitative takeaway grounded in the available evidence.


## I. Cross-Domain Universal Underlying Logic and Framework Patterns

### 1. Narrative Logic Chain Breakdown: The Final Synthesis

Top-tier Conclusion sections universally follow a **"Synthesis -> Impact -> Future"** logic, serving as the final takeaway for the reader. Unlike the Discussion (which argues and interprets), the Conclusion declares and summarizes.

| Order | Functional Module | Proportion | Core Writing Goal | Key Distinction from Discussion |
| :--- | :--- | :--- | :--- | :--- |
| **1** | **Restatement of Core Finding** | 30-40% | Concisely restate the primary answer to the research question without new data. | **Discussion**: Interprets *why* and *how*. **Conclusion**: States *what* was definitively found. |
| **2** | **Value & Impact Elevation** | 30-40% | Articulate the broader significance (theoretical, clinical, societal). | **Discussion**: Argues for significance with evidence. **Conclusion**: Declares significance as a final fact. |
| **3** | **Final Recommendation/Outlook** | 20-30% | Provide a final call to action or broad future direction. | **Discussion**: Details specific limitations & technical next steps. **Conclusion**: Broad visionary outlook. |

### 2. Core Functional Module Division

*   **Essential Information**:
    *   **Direct Answer**: A clear statement answering the hypothesis/research question posed in the Introduction.
    *   **Core Contribution**: One-sentence summary of the novel contribution.
    *   **Broad Implication**: How this changes the field, practice, or understanding.
    *   **Final Closing**: A memorable final sentence that resonates with the reader.

*   **Absolute Prohibitions**:
    *   **No New Data**: Never introduce numbers, statistics, or findings not presented in Results.
    *   **No Detailed Interpretation**: Do not re-explain mechanisms (belongs in Discussion).
    *   **No Extended Limitations**: Do not dedicate significant space to weaknesses (belongs in Discussion).
    *   **No Vague Generalities**: Avoid empty phrases like "more research is needed" without specific direction.

*   **Alignment with Introduction**:
    *   Must directly echo the research gap stated in the Introduction.
    *   Must confirm whether the initial hypothesis was supported.
    *   Must fulfill the promise of contribution made in the Introduction.

### 3. Language and Narrative Universal Norms

*   **Tense Selection**:
    *   **Primary**: Simple Present ("This study demonstrates," "Our findings suggest").
    *   **Secondary**: Simple Past (only when referring to specific actions taken, "We investigated").
    *   **Future**: Simple Future or Modals ("Future work will," "Should be explored").

*   **Voice Preference**:
    *   **Active Voice**: Preferred for clarity and authority ("We conclude," "This work establishes").
    *   **Concise Structure**: Sentences should be direct and impactful; avoid complex nested clauses.

*   **Tone and Style**:
    *   **Authoritative yet Hedged**: Confident in findings but careful not to overclaim ("Supports," "Indicates" vs. "Proves").
    *   **Synthesizing**: Focus on the "big picture" rather than granular details.
    *   **Finality**: The language should signal the end of the narrative arc.

## II. Linkage Logic and Boundary Patterns between Conclusion and Discussion

### 1. Core Boundary Distinction

| Feature | Discussion Module | Conclusion Module |
| :--- | :--- | :--- |
| **Primary Function** | Interpretation, Argumentation, Contextualization | Synthesis, Declaration, Elevation |
| **Content Focus** | *Why* it happened, *How* it fits in literature | *What* was found, *So What* it matters |
| **Detail Level** | High (specific mechanisms, specific comparisons) | Low (broad takeaways, general implications) |
| **Limitations** | Detailed analysis of weaknesses and biases | Brief mention only if relevant to final takeaway |
| **Future Work** | Specific technical next steps | Broad visionary directions or calls to action |
| **Length** | Typically longer (25-35% of manuscript) | Typically shorter (5-10% of manuscript) |

### 2. Linkage and Transition Techniques

*   **Logical Flow**: The Discussion should build the argument; the Conclusion should deliver the verdict.
*   **Transition Phrases**: Use clear signposts to indicate the shift from argument to summary.
    *   *"In conclusion,"*
    *   *"Taken together,"*
    *   *"Collectively, these findings indicate,"*
    *   *"In summary,"*
*   **Content Handoff**:
    *   **Discussion**: Ends with a specific recommendation for future research.
    *   **Conclusion**: Starts with a restatement of the main finding, then elevates to broader impact.
    *   **Avoid**: Do not let the Discussion fade out; ensure a clear break before the Conclusion header (if separate) or paragraph.

### 3. Linkage Pitfall Avoidance

*   **Avoid Repetition**: Do not copy-paste sentences from the Discussion opening into the Conclusion. Rephrase for synthesis.
*   **Avoid Disconnect**: Ensure the Conclusion answers the specific questions raised in the Introduction and discussed in the Discussion.
*   **Avoid New Arguments**: Do not introduce new interpretive arguments in the Conclusion that were not vetted in the Discussion.
*   **Avoid Abrupt Ending**: Ensure the Conclusion feels like a natural culmination of the Discussion, not an afterthought.

## III. Differentiated Writing Patterns by Research Type

### 1. Specific Norms by Research Type

#### A. Clinical/Public Health Medical Research
*   **Focus**: Patient outcomes, guideline changes, public health impact.
*   **Conclusion Emphasis**: Direct recommendation for practice or policy.
*   **Linkage**: Discussion analyzes safety/efficacy; Conclusion states whether to adopt the intervention.
*   **Example Focus**: "These data support the integration of X into standard care for Y."

#### B. Experimental Basic Science
*   **Focus**: Mechanistic understanding, biological pathways, fundamental knowledge.
*   **Conclusion Emphasis**: New conceptual model or pathway identification.
*   **Linkage**: Discussion details molecular mechanisms; Conclusion states the broader biological principle revealed.
*   **Example Focus**: "This work establishes X as a key regulator of Y process."

#### C. Computer Science/AI/Algorithm
*   **Focus**: Performance, scalability, deployment potential, methodological contribution.
*   **Conclusion Emphasis**: Utility of the method and potential for adoption.
*   **Linkage**: Discussion compares benchmarks; Conclusion asserts the method's value for the community.
*   **Example Focus**: "Our framework provides a scalable solution for Z challenge."

#### D. Social Sciences/SSCI
*   **Focus**: Theoretical contribution, policy implication, societal understanding.
*   **Conclusion Emphasis**: Impact on theory or social policy.
*   **Linkage**: Discussion debates theoretical nuances; Conclusion states the refined theoretical position.
*   **Example Focus**: "These findings necessitate a revision of current theory regarding X."

#### E. Engineering
*   **Focus**: Validation, practical application, efficiency gains, safety.
*   **Conclusion Emphasis**: Readiness for implementation or design improvement.
*   **Linkage**: Discussion analyzes error margins; Conclusion confirms reliability for application.
*   **Example Focus**: "This design offers a robust alternative for high-load scenarios."

### 2. Core Differences Comparison Table

| Dimension | Clinical/Medical | Basic Science | CS/AI/Tech | Social Sciences (SSCI) | Engineering |
| :--- | :--- | :--- | :--- | :--- | :--- |
| **Conclusion Focus** | Practice/Policy Change | Conceptual Model | Method Utility | Theory/Policy Impact | Implementation/Design |
| **Tone** | Prescriptive/Cautious | Declarative/Insightful | Confident/Technical | Nuanced/Argumentative | Practical/Validating |
| **Future Outlook** | Larger Trials/Populations | Mechanistic Depth | Scalability/Integration | Longitudinal/Contextual | Optimization/Scaling |
| **Discussion Link** | Efficacy -> Recommendation | Mechanism -> Principle | Benchmark -> Adoption | Analysis -> Theory | Validation -> Application |
