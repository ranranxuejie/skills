
## IV. Pitfall Avoidance Guide & Red Lines

### 1. High-Frequency Errors
*   **Error 1: The "Data Dump" Introduction**.
    *   *Issue*: Listing too many specific results or detailed statistical values.
    *   *Fix*: Keep it conceptual. Summarize the *implication* of the result, not the number itself.
*   **Error 2: The "Missing Gap"**.
    *   *Issue*: Jumping from background directly to "We did X" without explaining *why* X was needed.
    *   *Fix*: Insert a dedicated paragraph or sentences explicitly stating the limitation of current knowledge.
*   **Error 3: Over-citing or Under-citing**.
    *   *Issue*: Citing every single paper (clutter) or ignoring key seminal works (ignorance).
    *   *Fix*: Cite representative reviews and key primary papers. Group citations where appropriate ("[1-5]").

### 2. Adaptation: Comprehensive vs. Specialized Journals
*   **Nature/Science/Cell**:
    *   **Style**: Broad appeal. Minimize jargon. Explain *why* a non-specialist should care.
    *   **Structure**: Often merges the "Gap" and "Objective" into a flowing narrative without rigid sub-headings.
*   **Specialized Journals (e.g., *PRL*, *Lancet Oncology*, *IEEE TPAMI*)**:
    *   **Style**: Technical precision allowed. Assume reader expertise.
    *   **Structure**: Can be more direct. Explicitly state the technical gap. Roadmap paragraphs are more acceptable.

### 3. Absolute Red Lines (Strictly Forbidden)
*   **Plagiarism**: Copying sentences or structures from other papers without paraphrasing.
*   **Self-Promotion**: Using adjectives like "groundbreaking," "revolutionary," or "perfect." Let the data speak.
*   **Irrelevant Background**: Including history or facts that do not directly lead to the research gap.
*   **New Data in Intro**: Introducing data/figures that are not discussed in the Results section.
*   **Informal Tone**: Using "I," "we feel," slang, or rhetorical questions (unless used very sparingly for effect in specific fields).

## V. Top-Tier Introduction Quality Checklist

Before submission, verify your Introduction against these criteria:

### 1. Completeness
- [ ] Does the opening clearly establish the broad importance of the field?
- [ ] Is the relevant literature reviewed sufficiently to set the stage?
- [ ] Is the specific research gap (the "niche") explicitly identified?
- [ ] Are the study objectives/hypotheses clearly stated?
- [ ] Is the main contribution/significance summarized?

### 2. Logic
- [ ] Does the narrative flow logically from Broad -> Narrow (Funnel shape)?
- [ ] Is there a clear causal link between the "Gap" and the "Objective"?
- [ ] Do transitions between paragraphs guide the reader smoothly?
- [ ] Does the introduction avoid jumping back and forth between topics?

### 3. Rigor
- [ ] Are claims supported by appropriate citations?
- [ ] Is the language hedged appropriately (avoiding overstatement)?
- [ ] Are tenses used correctly (Present for truths, Past for previous studies, Present for current work)?
- [ ] Are abbreviations defined upon first use?

### 4. Norms
- [ ] Is the word count within the journal's limit (typically 10-15% of total manuscript)?
- [ ] Are there no detailed method descriptions or raw data lists?
- [ ] Is the tone professional, objective, and confident but not arrogant?
- [ ] Does it adhere to the specific stylistic preferences of the target journal (e.g., broad vs. technical)?
