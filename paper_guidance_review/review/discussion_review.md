
## IV. Pitfall Avoidance Guide & Red Lines

### 1. High-Frequency Errors

*   **Error 1: Results Repetition**.
    *   *Issue*: Restating specific numbers, p-values, or statistics from Results.
    *   *Fix*: Summarize findings qualitatively without data; focus on meaning.
    *   *Example*: "The OR was 2.3 (95% CI 1.5-3.5)" → ✅ "There was a significant positive association"

*   **Error 2: Over-interpretation**.
    *   *Issue*: Making claims beyond what the data can support (causality from correlation).
    *   *Fix*: Use appropriate hedging; acknowledge alternative explanations.
    *   *Example*: "X causes Y" → ✅ "X is associated with Y; causal inference requires further study"

*   **Error 3: Literature Dump Without Dialogue**.
    *   *Issue*: Listing citations without meaningful comparison or critique.
    *   *Fix*: Explicitly state how your work confirms, contradicts, or extends each cited study.
    *   *Example*: "Smith et al. found X. Jones et al. found Y." → ✅ "While Smith et al. reported X, our findings of Y suggest..."

*   **Error 4: Hidden or Minimized Limitations**.
    *   *Issue*: Burying limitations in one sentence or omitting major weaknesses.
    *   *Fix*: Dedicate a substantive paragraph to limitations with honest assessment.
    *   *Example*: "Some limitations exist." → ✅ "Three key limitations warrant discussion: first..."

*   **Error 5: Unsubstantiated Clinical/Commercial Promises**.
    *   *Issue*: Claiming immediate clinical or commercial applicability without evidence.
    *   *Fix*: Frame applications as potential; acknowledge validation needed.
    *   *Example*: "This will transform clinical practice" → ✅ "This has potential implications for clinical practice pending further validation"

*   **Error 6: Disconnected from Research Question**.
    *   *Issue*: Discussion drifts away from the original hypothesis or research question.
    *   *Fix*: Begin and end by explicitly addressing the research question from Introduction.
    *   *Example*: Ensure opening paragraph directly answers the Introduction's research question.

*   **Error 7: Introducing New Results**.
    *   *Issue*: Presenting data or findings not shown in Results section.
    *   *Fix*: All interpretive claims must be grounded in presented Results.
    *   *Example*: If discussing a subgroup, ensure that subgroup analysis is in Results.

### 2. Adaptation: Comprehensive vs. Specialized Journals

*   **Nature/Science/Cell**:
    *   **Style**: Narrative-driven, broader implications emphasized.
    *   **Length**: Often shorter (4-6 paragraphs); more focus on significance.
    *   **Focus**: Why this matters to the world, not just the field.
    *   **Limitations**: May be briefer; emphasis on breakthrough nature.
    *   **Future Directions**: Often integrated into conclusion rather than separate section.

*   **Specialized Journals (e.g., *PLOS ONE*, *JAMA*, *IEEE Transactions*)**:
    *   **Style**: Structured, detailed, technically precise.
    *   **Length**: Can be longer (6-10 paragraphs); more comprehensive coverage.
    *   **Focus**: Field-specific contributions and technical implications.
    *   **Limitations**: More detailed; often required by reporting guidelines.
    *   **Future Directions**: Often a dedicated subsection with specific recommendations.

### 3. Absolute Red Lines (Strictly Forbidden)

*   **Data/Result Fabrication in Discussion**: Claiming findings not actually observed or presented.
*   **Citation Manipulation**: Citing literature that doesn't support the claimed comparison.
*   **Plagiarism**: Copying discussion structure or phrasing from other papers.
*   **Undisclosed Conflicts**: Making recommendations that benefit undisclosed financial interests.
*   **Clinical Harm**: Making treatment recommendations beyond the study's evidence level.
*   **HARKing in Discussion**: Presenting post-hoc interpretations as pre-specified hypotheses.
*   **Disparaging Competitors**: Inappropriately criticizing other researchers' work without evidence.
*   **Overstating Novelty**: Claiming "first" without thorough literature verification.
*   **Ignoring Contradictory Evidence**: Dismissing conflicting studies without engagement.
*   **Promotional Language**: Using marketing or commercial language ("best", "superior", "unmatched").

## V. Top-Tier Discussion Section Quality Checklist

Before submission, verify your Discussion section against these criteria:

### 1. Boundary Clarity (Discussion vs. Results)
- [ ] Are there no specific statistics, p-values, or raw data presented?
- [ ] Is the content interpretive rather than descriptive?
- [ ] Are all claims grounded in Results section findings?
- [ ] Is there no methodological detail (belongs in Methods)?
- [ ] Does the section avoid figure/table references (except for summary figures)?

### 2. Logical Completeness
- [ ] Does the opening paragraph directly address the research question from Introduction?
- [ ] Is there a clear progression from findings → interpretation → implications → limitations → conclusion?
- [ ] Does each paragraph have one main interpretive point?
- [ ] Are transitions between paragraphs smooth and logical?
- [ ] Does the conclusion synthesize the main takeaway without introducing new ideas?

### 3. Interpretation Depth
- [ ] Are mechanisms or theoretical explanations provided for key findings?
- [ ] Is there meaningful dialogue with at least 5-10 key prior studies?
- [ ] Are contradictions with prior literature addressed honestly?
- [ ] Are alternative explanations for findings considered?
- [ ] Is the "so what?" question clearly answered (why does this matter)?

### 4. Rigor & Honesty
- [ ] Are limitations acknowledged transparently (minimum 1 paragraph)?
- [ ] Is appropriate hedging used for speculative claims (may, suggests, could)?
- [ ] Are causal claims avoided (unless supported by RCT design)?
- [ ] Are absolute statements (proves, definitely, always) eliminated?
- [ ] Is the strength of claims proportional to the strength of evidence?
- [ ] Are non-significant or unexpected findings addressed honestly?

### 5. Normative Compliance
- [ ] Is the word count within journal limits (typically 15-25% of manuscript)?
- [ ] Are tenses used correctly (Past for findings, Present for interpretation, Future for directions)?
- [ ] Are citations accurate and do they support the claimed comparisons?
- [ ] Is the tone professional without promotional or emotional language?
- [ ] Are implications appropriate for the study design (no over-generalization)?
- [ ] Does the section end with a strong, memorable concluding statement?
- [ ] Are future directions specific and actionable (not vague "more research is needed")?
